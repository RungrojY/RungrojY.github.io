1.1 Mathematical formulations

The definitions of the \(i^\mathrm{th}\) moments for propagating radical and dead polymer in radical polymerization are given by \(Y_i\) and \(Q_i\) in the following equations. The same concept can be extended to other polymer populations, such as dormant chains, intermediate radical chains, and others. In most cases, due to their highly reactive nature, the propagating radical moments are much smaller than those of dead chains and other polymer chain populations (equivalent to quasi-steady state assumption, QSSA). Therefore, the overall polymer moments can be assumed to be equal to the dead chain moments for systems involving only dead and radical chains (\(Y_i \ll Q_i\) and thus \(Y_i + Q_i \cong Q_i\))

\[\begin{align*} Y_i &\equiv \sum_{r=0}^{\infty} r^i [P_r^{\bullet}] \\ Q_i &\equiv \sum_{r=0}^{\infty} r^i [P_r] \\ \end{align*}\]

From the definition, it can be seen that the zeroth order moment physically represents the concentration of polymer chains in the system. On the other hand, the first order moment represents the concentration of monomeric units that have been incorporated into the polymer chains. The zeroth and first moments can be related to the number-fraction and weight-fraction molecular weight distribution (or equivalently chain length distribution) of polymer chains, \(n(r)\) and \(w(r)\), according to

\[\begin{align*} n(r) &= \frac{[P_r]}{\sum_{r=0}^{\infty} [P_r]} = \frac{[P_r]}{Q_0} \\ w(r) &= \frac{r [P_r]}{\sum_{r=0}^{\infty} r [P_r]} = \frac{r [P_r]}{Q_1} \\ \end{align*}\]

It should be noted that the two distributions are not independent of each other, as knowledge of either distribution allows calculation of the other using the following relations:

\[\begin{align*} n(r) &= \frac{[w(r)/r]}{\sum_{r=1}^{\infty} [w(r)/r]} \quad \text{and} \\ w(r) &= \frac{[r \times n(r)]}{\sum_{r=1}^{\infty} [r \times n(r)]} \quad . \end{align*}\]

Base on Equation above, the number- and weight-average chain length (\(\hat{r}_n\) and \(\hat{r}_w\)) can be related to the moments as given by Equation (5) and (6), respectively. The number- and weight- average molecular weight can be obtained by multiplying the corresponding average chain length with the molar mass of the repeating unit. Based on its definition, polydispersity index (\(PDI\)) can also be expressed as a function of the moments, shown by Equation (7). Equation (3)-(7) are applicable for systems with only dead and radical polymer chains by applying QSSA to the radical concentration. If other polymer population is involved or if QSSA is not applicable, these equations should be rewritten to account for their contributions to the overall distribution.

\[\begin{align*} \bar{r}_n &\equiv \sum_{r=0}^{\infty} [ r \times n(r) ] = \frac{Q_1}{Q_0} \\ \bar{r}_w &\equiv \sum_{r=0}^{\infty} [ r \times w(r) ] = \frac{Q_2}{Q_1} \\ PDI &\equiv \frac{\bar{r}_w}{\bar{r}_n} = \frac{Q_2 Q_0}{(Q_1)^2} \end{align*}\]

Several examples are presented to elucidate the concepts explained in the Mathematical Formulation section and to demonstrate the versatility of method of moments in modeling various polymerization processes. The derivations presented here are obtained based on the reaction mechanisms listed. Modifications to include other side reactions, such as chain transfer to monomer, solvent, etc., are straightforward, and can be included when deemed necessary. Moreover, the calculation is limited up to the second order moment. The second order moment suffices in most cases since it allows calculation of number- and weight-average chain length (thus also giving polydispersity index). However, when required, the higher order moments can be obtained by extending the derivation in a similar fashion. For simplicity purpose, the change in reaction rate constants (due to china length dependence. “gell effect”, glass effect, etc.) is not considered in these examples. Reader are encouraged to read the various methods available in the literature to include these variations.

1.2 Free radical polymerization (FRP)

Free radical polymerization (FRP) is commonly employed in industrial settings owing to its ability to polymerize a wide range of monomers. Basic reactions involved in FRP are shown in Scheme 1 and listed below (\(r,s=0,1,2,\ldots,\infty\)).

\[\begin{alignat*}{3} &\text{Initiation} &&\quad I \xrightarrow{f,\ k_d} 2P_0^{\bullet} &&\quad \\ % &\text{Propagation} &&\quad P_r^{\bullet} + M \xrightarrow{k_p} P_{r+1}^{\bullet} &&\quad \\ % &\text{Termination} &&\quad P_r^{\bullet} + P_s^{\bullet} \xrightarrow{k_{td}} P_r + P_s &&\quad \text{by disproportionation} \\ % & &&\quad P_r^{\bullet} + P_s^{\bullet} \xrightarrow{k_{tc}} P_{r+s} &&\quad \text{by combination (coupling)} \end{alignat*}\]
